SOC2 Type I vs Type II: Which Do You Need?

One of the first questions companies ask when pursuing SOC2 is: "Should I get Type I or Type II?" The answer depends on your sales cycle, customer requirements, and timeline. Let's break it down.

The Quick Answer

Type I

Point-in-time assessment

Verifies that your controls are properly designed at a specific moment.

  • ‚úÖ Less expensive: $15-20k

Type II

Operating effectiveness over time

Verifies that your controls work effectively over 3-12 months.

  • ‚úÖ Gold standard certification
  • ‚úÖ Accepted by all enterprises

Choose Type I If...

‚úÖ You need certification ASAP

You have a deal closing in 30-60 days and need to show compliance quickly. Type I lets you get certified in weeks, not months.

‚úÖ Your controls are newly implemented

If you just set up your security controls, you don't have months of historical evidence yet. Type I is perfect for this stage.

‚úÖ You're targeting SMB customers

Many small and mid-market customers accept Type I reports, especially if you're early-stage. You can upgrade to Type II for your next renewal.

‚úÖ Budget is tight

Type I costs $10-15k less than Type II. If you need to conserve cash, start with Type I and upgrade later once revenue grows.

‚úÖ You want to test your readiness

Type I serves as a "practice run" before committing to the longer Type II observation period. It identifies gaps without the lengthy timeline.

Choose Type II If...

‚úÖ Customers specifically require it

Many large enterprises, financial institutions, and healthcare companies only accept Type II. Check your security questionnaires.

‚úÖ You have time before you need it

If your first enterprise deal is 9-12 months away, go straight for Type II. No point getting Type I if you'll immediately need to upgrade.

‚úÖ Your controls have been operating for 3+ months

If your security program is mature and you have historical evidence, there's no reason not to go for Type II immediately.

‚úÖ You're in a competitive market

Type II gives you a competitive advantage. It signals to prospects that your security posture is mature and battle-tested.

‚úÖ You want to minimize audit fatigue

Getting Type II once is better than Type I now + Type II later. You'll go through the audit process once instead of twice.

Decision Framework

Ask yourself these questions:

1. What are your customers asking for?

Check your security questionnaires and RFPs. Do they specifically say "Type II"?

2. When do you need certification?

Less than 3 months = Type I. More than 6 months = Type II.

3. How mature is your security program?

New controls (less than 3 months) = Type I. Established controls (3+ months) = Type II.

4. What's your budget?

Under $20k = Type I. $25k+ available = Type II.

5. Who are you selling to?

SMB/Mid-market = Type I works. Enterprise/Regulated = Need Type II.

Common Misconceptions

‚ùå "Type I is worthless"

False. Many companies close millions in deals with Type I. It's a legitimate certification that proves your controls are properly designed.

‚ùå "You must get Type II"

Not always. If your customers accept Type I and you need certification quickly, it's the smart choice. You can always upgrade later.

‚ùå "Type II takes a full year"

The observation period can be 3, 6, 9, or 12 months. Many companies choose 6-month Type II audits to balance rigor with speed.

‚ùå "Type I to Type II means two separate audits"

You can use the same auditor and much of the same documentation. The Type II audit builds on your Type I foundation.

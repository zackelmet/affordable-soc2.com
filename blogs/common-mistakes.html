5 Common SOC2 Mistakes That Delay Certification

Avoid these costly pitfalls that can add months to your compliance timeline.

1

Starting the Audit Before You're Ready

Average Delay: 3-6 months

The Mistake: Companies engage an auditor, start the formal audit, and then discover major gaps that require months to remediate. Meanwhile, the clock is ticking on audit fees.

Why It Happens: Eagerness to get certified, pressure from sales, or underestimating the work required.

Real Example: A Series A startup engaged a Big 4 auditor without a readiness assessment. Three weeks into the audit, the auditor identified 47 control gaps. The audit had to pause for 4 months while they remediated issues‚Äîcosting an extra $25,000 in extended audit fees.

‚úÖ How to Avoid This:

  • ‚Ä¢ Do a thorough readiness assessment 2-3 months before engaging an auditor
  • ‚Ä¢ Use a gap analysis tool to identify issues early
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't schedule audit fieldwork until all gaps are closed
  • ‚Ä¢ Run a mock audit internally first
2

Poor Documentation and Evidence Management

Average Delay: 2-4 months

The Mistake: Waiting until the audit to gather evidence, then scrambling to find logs, screenshots, and documentation across multiple systems. Evidence is incomplete or doesn't cover the required timeframe.

Why It Happens: Not understanding what evidence is needed or thinking it can be gathered "later."

Real Example: A B2B SaaS company realized mid-audit that they hadn't saved firewall logs for the full observation period. They had to extend their audit by 6 months to collect the required evidence.

‚úÖ How to Avoid This:

  • ‚Ä¢ Start collecting evidence from day one of control implementation
  • ‚Ä¢ Use automated evidence collection tools
  • ‚Ä¢ Create a shared folder structure for audit evidence
  • ‚Ä¢ Maintain a spreadsheet mapping controls to evidence
  • ‚Ä¢ Enable logging with proper retention (90+ days) early

3

Over-Scoping the Initial Audit

Average Delay: 1-3 months

The Mistake: Including too many systems, all five Trust Service Criteria, or non-essential environments in your first audit scope. This multiplies the work and complexity.

Why It Happens: Wanting to be "thorough" or not understanding that scope can be expanded in future audits.

Real Example: A startup scoped their entire tech stack including development, staging, and production environments. The audit took 8 months when it should have taken 3. They could have scoped just production for the first audit.

‚úÖ How to Avoid This:

  • ‚Ä¢ Start with Security + one additional TSC (usually Availability or Confidentiality)
  • ‚Ä¢ Scope only your production environment, not dev/staging
  • ‚Ä¢ Focus on customer-facing systems first
  • ‚Ä¢ Exclude non-critical SaaS tools from initial scope
  • ‚Ä¢ You can always expand scope in your next annual audit
4

Neglecting Vendor Risk Management

Average Delay: 1-2 months

The Mistake: Forgetting that third-party vendors need to be assessed for security risks. During the audit, you realize you haven't reviewed vendor security or obtained SOC2 reports from critical providers.

Why It Happens: Not understanding that vendor security is your responsibility, or underestimating how many vendors you use.

Real Example: Mid-audit, a company discovered they couldn't get SOC2 reports from two critical vendors (one for payment processing, one for data storage). They had to find alternative vendors and migrate systems‚Äîdelaying certification by 3 months.

‚úÖ How to Avoid This:

  • ‚Ä¢ Create a vendor inventory early (spreadsheet is fine)
  • ‚Ä¢ Identify vendors that handle customer data
  • ‚Ä¢ Request SOC2/ISO 27001 reports from critical vendors
  • ‚Ä¢ For vendors without reports, conduct security questionnaires
  • ‚Ä¢ Consider vendor risk management tools for ongoing monitoring

5

Treating SOC2 as a One-Time Project

Average Impact: Audit failures or surveillance findings

The Mistake: Implementing controls just to pass the audit, then letting them lapse. During Type II observation or annual renewal, auditors find that controls aren't consistently operating.

Why It Happens: Viewing compliance as a checkbox instead of an ongoing program. Not assigning ownership or accountability.

Real Example: A company passed their Type I audit but failed their Type II because they stopped conducting quarterly access reviews after month 2. The auditor found excessive permissions throughout the observation period.

‚úÖ How to Avoid This:

  • ‚Ä¢ Assign a compliance owner (doesn't have to be full-time)
  • ‚Ä¢ Create a compliance calendar with recurring tasks
  • ‚Ä¢ Use automation to reduce manual work
  • ‚Ä¢ Make compliance part of your engineering culture
  • ‚Ä¢ Schedule quarterly internal compliance reviews
  • ‚Ä¢ Treat SOC2 as ongoing security hygiene, not a certification chase
